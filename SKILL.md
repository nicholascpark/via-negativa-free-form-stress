# Via Negativa Free-Form Stress

Two modes, one method: **Reflective** (the thinking isn't broken — what absent thought will become the failure point?) and **Diagnostic** (the thinking IS the problem — why does it loop, stall, or avoid?). Both use via-negativa perception and frame analysis. The skill asks: **"What are you NOT thinking, and does that absence matter?"**

## Flow

**Reflective**: Step 0 (Intake) → Step Q (Elicitation) → Layers → Step R (Response Reading).
**Diagnostic**: Step D (Diagnostic Intake) → Step Q (Elicitation) → Layers → Step R (Response Reading).

### Depth levels

- **Quick check** → Layers 1–2 (fast but less differentiated)
- **Stress test** → Layers 1–3 (default — includes frame analysis, where differentiating insight lives)
- **Full via negativa** → All four layers (includes deterministic synthesis)
- **Deep reflection** → All four layers + Layer 4b stochastic perturbation protocol

**Deep reflection triggers**: The thinker says "take your time," "go deeper," or "deep reflection." OR the agent detects that Layer 4a synthesis feels thin and asks: "Can I take more time to search?" If the thinker agrees, Layer 4b activates.

---

## Step 0: Intake — Identify Thinking Type

Determine what kind of thinking you're examining. Read each type accordingly:

**A plan** (roadmap, strategy, outline, to-do, timeline):
Read BETWEEN the lines. Ordering reveals priorities. Granularity reveals confidence (detailed = confident, vague = uncertain). What's at the bottom or absent reveals what the thinker considers unimportant or hasn't considered.

**A draft or document** (essay, blog post, proposal, notes, journal):
A snapshot of thinking. Where writing is fluent = confident; halting/hedged/vague = uncertain or avoiding. Transitions reveal reasoning structure; missing transitions reveal reasoning gaps.

**A verbal idea** (conversation, brainstorm, spoken plan):
The conversation IS the artifact. What's said first = top-of-mind. What's mentioned only when asked = background. What's never mentioned = frame boundaries. Questions you must ask are themselves Layer 1 findings.

**A decision or priority list**:
Most revealing type. Every inclusion implies an exclusion. Read for what's ON it, then for what's NOT on it and what that absence reveals.

**A mental model or framework**:
Highest-leverage target. Analyze it the way Layer 3 analyzes implicit frames — what does this model make visible, and what does it structurally render invisible?

**Notes or fragments** (scattered thoughts, bullet points):
Most honest artifact — notes don't perform coherence. Look for clusters (what the thinker keeps returning to), isolates (unconnected thoughts), and contradictions (thoughts that can't both be true but coexist without resolution).

## Step D: Diagnostic Intake (stuck / circular thinking)

Not "What should you do next?" but "What's missing from your thinking that would make 'what to do next' obvious?"

### Diagnostic acquisition:

1. **Understand the stuck point**: Where does the thinking stop, loop, or stall? Get the shape of the stuckness — not just "I'm stuck" but "I'm stuck HERE, in THIS way."

2. **Map the thinking loop**: If circular, trace it. Each step in the circle avoids a thought that would break out of it.

3. **Find the structural gap**: What's ABSENT from the thinker's cognitive toolkit that would dissolve the entire class of stuck points?
   - Missing distinction? (treating two different things as one)
   - Missing permission? (won't let themselves think X)
   - Missing information? (a question they haven't asked)
   - Missing frame? (the problem needs a different kind of thinking)

4. **Identify the avoidance**: What thought is the thinker actively not having? Not what they forgot — what they're steering around. The loop often exists to avoid a conclusion the thinker doesn't want to reach. Surface with care but surface it.

5. **Find the recursive pattern**: Has this kind of stuckness happened before? If so, the structural absence is in the thinker's repertoire, not in this plan.

### Diagnostic lens for each layer:

- **Layer 1**: "What concept, distinction, or question is missing from your cognitive toolkit for this problem?"
- **Layer 2**: "What assumption about yourself or the situation is keeping you stuck?" Distinguish external assumptions from internal ones ("I should be able to figure this out alone," "I can't change X").
- **Layer 3**: "Is the thinking frame itself the problem?" Planning when they should be exploring. Analyzing when they should be deciding. Optimizing when the question is whether to do it at all.
- **Layer 4**: "What kind of thinker would you need to become to make this problem dissolve?"

The profound diagnostic output is the moment the thinker sees the structure — e.g., "You're not stuck on this plan. You're stuck because you're using planning to avoid a decision you've already made but won't admit to yourself." The next thought becomes obvious once the structure is visible.

## Step Q: Elicitation — Ask Before Telling

Before running layers, ask 2–3 diagnostic questions designed to reveal the shape of the thinking from the thinker's own responses. Not clarifying questions — questions the thinker can't answer.

### Why ask first

1. **The answer reveals more than the artifact.** What the thinker says fluently vs. where they stumble, hedge, or go silent tells you where the frame's edges are.
2. **The thinker generates their own insight.** Being asked "what happens on the day this plan stops working?" and discovering you have no answer is a revelation. Being told "your plan has no failure mode" is feedback.
3. **It calibrates depth.** Responses reveal whether Layer 3 will land or whether they're already frame-aware. Push past what they've already reached.

### Elicitation questions by mode

**Reflective** (stress testing a plan or idea):
- "What's the first thing you expect to go wrong?" — Clear answer = some resilience. No answer = Layer 1 will hit hard.
- "What would make you abandon this entirely?" — Tests failure criteria and commitment clarity. Hesitation is diagnostic.
- "Who disagrees with this, and what's the strongest version of their objection?" — Tests genuine engagement with opposing views.

**Diagnostic** (stuck or circular thinking):
- "What happens right before you get stuck?" — The moment before the loop restarts is where the avoided thought lives.
- "If you had to act right now with no more thinking, what would you do?" — Separates genuine uncertainty from thinking-as-avoidance. Immediate answer = the thinking is the obstacle.
- "What are you afraid of finding out?" — Direct avoidance probe. The thinker's reaction — not their answer — is often the most diagnostic signal.

### Reading the responses

- **Fluent, immediate**: They've thought about this. Push past it.
- **Hesitation, hedging**: You've found an edge. Focus layers here.
- **Deflection or subject change**: You've found the avoidance. Handle with care but don't let it pass unmarked.
- **Irritation or dismissal**: Highest-signal response. The thinker is protecting something. This is data, not confirmation — they might be right. But note it.

### Elicitation is non-optional

When there is a live thinker, always run elicitation before layers. Without it, Layer 3 findings reliably collapse into catalog entries — structurally correct, experientially hollow.

Exception: reviewing a document with no live thinker. Run layers cold but flag the ceiling: cold analysis pattern-matches; live elicitation reveals.

## Layer 1: Absence Inventory

**Question: "What is not being thought that could be?"**

### Five universal probes:

1. **Failure probe**: "What happens when this doesn't work?"
   → No plan B, no failure criteria, no definition of "this isn't working."

2. **Boundary probe**: "Where does this thinking end and something else begin?"
   → Undefined scope, no distinction between what the thinker controls and what they don't.

3. **Lifecycle probe**: "What happens before, during, and after?"
   → Plans that start in the middle or have no endpoint, no consideration of sustainability.

4. **Stakeholder probe**: "Who else is affected or needed?"
   → Plans that assume the thinker acts alone, missing dependencies on others.

5. **Evidence probe**: "What beliefs here are untested?"
   → Assumptions presented as facts, reasoning from a single example.

### Thinking-specific accelerators:

- **Emotional absences**: Reasoning without acknowledging feelings about the situation. Emotion is data being excluded.
- **Temporal absences**: Planning for a single moment without considering before and after.
- **Energy absences**: Does the plan account for actual capacity, or assume infinite energy?
- **Constraint absences**: Has the thinker named what they can't change?

### Output format:
Run every candidate through the Relevance Gate (see Usage Notes). Then:
- **What's not being thought** (specific, not vague)
- **Risk if unthought** (concrete scenario, one sentence)
- **Severity** (critical / significant / minor)

Aim for 3–7 findings. Fewer is better if fewer is honest.

## Layer 2: Load-Bearing Assumptions

**Question: "What invisible premises is this thinking standing on?"**

Unstated beliefs the reasoning requires to be true. More dangerous than missing thoughts because they're structural — the thinker can't see them because they're the lens, not the thing being looked at.

### Method:
1. For each major element, ask: "What must be true about the world, the thinker, or other people for this to make sense?"
2. Make implicit assumptions explicit.
3. Classify each:
   - **Validated**: Evidence exists
   - **Plausible**: Reasonable but unverified
   - **Fragile**: Could easily become false
   - **Invisible**: The thinker likely doesn't know they're assuming this

### Assumption categories:

- **Self-assumptions**: "I'll follow through," "I'll feel the same way tomorrow," "I can handle this," "I know what I want"
- **World-assumptions**: "Things will stay roughly the same," "this opportunity will still be there," "my information is sufficient"
- **People-assumptions**: "They'll understand," "they'll agree," "they'll do their part," "their intentions are what they appear to be"
- **Temporal assumptions**: "There's enough time," "the order doesn't matter," "I can do this later"
- **Frame assumptions**: "This is the kind of problem I think it is," "the question I'm answering is the right question"

### Self-assumptions are the most dangerous category

Plans are disproportionately shaped by invisible self-assumptions. Surface them with precision but without judgment — make them visible, not undermining.

### Output format:
- **The assumption** (stated explicitly)
- **Where it's load-bearing** (what breaks if it's wrong)
- **Fragility rating** (robust / moderate / fragile)
- **How to validate or hedge** (actionable next step)

## Layer 3: Frame Exclusions

**Question: "What does the way you're thinking make impossible to see?"**

Layers 1–2 find things the thinker missed. Layer 3 finds things the thinker **could not have seen** from inside their current frame.

### Method:
1. **Identify the frame**: What paradigm, mental model, or cognitive habit shaped this reasoning? (See `references/frame-catalog.md` for common frames and their blindspots.)
2. **Name the frame's blindspots**: What does this way of thinking structurally exclude?
3. **Consider alternative frames**: What would a different way of thinking reveal?
4. **Assess frame-thought fit**: Is this the right kind of thinking for this problem, or was the frame chosen by habit?

### When Layer 3 will and won't produce value

**High-value**: Life decisions where thinker feels stuck (frame IS the stuckness), plans that keep failing the same way (frame-problem mismatch), thinking that feels productive but never concludes (analysis as avoidance).

**Low-value**: Clear tactical thinking about well-understood problems, explicitly chosen appropriate frames, simple decisions with clear criteria.

**The test**: Before writing a finding, ask: "Would the thinker say 'I hadn't realized I was thinking about it that way' or 'obviously, that's how I'm thinking about it'?" If the latter, skip.

### The catalog trap

The frame catalog (`references/frame-catalog.md`) exists for calibration, not lookup. The failure mode: read artifact → match to nearest catalog entry → report that entry's blindspots. This produces output that is structurally correct and completely generic.

To avoid this:
1. **Describe the frame in the thinker's own language first.** "You're treating this relationship like a project with deliverables" is a revelation. "You're in a planning frame" is a label.
2. **The catalog is a sanity check, not a source.** The finding should sound discovered from this artifact, not retrieved from a reference file.
3. **If your finding could appear unchanged in a different thinker's analysis, it's too generic.** Aim for specificity to THIS thinker's problem.

### Output format:
- **Frame identified** (the cognitive paradigm in use)
- **What this frame illuminates** (its strengths — be fair)
- **What this frame structurally excludes** (its necessary blindspots)
- **Alternative frame** (a different way of thinking that would reveal something new)
- **Recommendation** (is a frame shift warranted, or just awareness?)

## Layer 4: Via Negativa Design (Generative Metacognition)

**Question: "What does the shape of your not-thinking reveal about what you're actually trying to think?"**

After Layers 1–3, ask: does the pattern of what's NOT being thought have a coherent shape? Often the negative space reveals a latent thought the thinker is reaching toward but hasn't articulated.

Two sub-protocols:
- **Layer 4a** (deterministic synthesis) — always runs at this depth
- **Layer 4b** (stochastic perturbation) — runs only at "Deep reflection" depth or by explicit trigger

---

### Layer 4a: Deterministic Synthesis

#### Method:
1. Look across all findings from Layers 1–3
2. Ask: "If I built a thought to fill exactly this negative space, what would it be?"
3. Ask: "What kind of thinker would need to exist for none of these absences to be absences?"
4. Propose the smallest cognitive shift that addresses the most critical gaps
5. Frame it as a new question, not a new answer — the thinker should think the thought themselves

#### Output format:
- **Pattern in the negative space** (what the absences collectively suggest about the thinker's relationship to this problem)
- **The latent thought** (what the thinker is reaching toward but hasn't articulated)
- **The reframing question** (a single question that, if sat with, would dissolve the most important absences)
- **What to resist thinking** (not every absent thought should be added — some absences are wisdom)

---

### Layer 4b: Stochastic Perturbation Protocol

**Purpose**: Deterministic synthesis is bounded by the analyst's associative range. Layer 4b introduces controlled randomness — Monte Carlo sampling of conceptual space — to surface insights the deterministic pass structurally cannot reach.

4b runs AFTER 4a, using 4a's output and all Layer 1–3 findings as input.

#### Stage 1: Entropy Extraction (the seed)

Derive pseudo-random parameters from the thinker's artifact text. The text's statistical properties are semantically uncorrelated with concept generation, making them a genuine entropy source.

**Character-level features:**
- Count vowels in the first sentence, mod 7
- Consonant-to-vowel ratio (rounded to nearest integer)
- Shannon entropy of character distribution

**Word-level features:**
- Length of the Nth word (where N = sentence count)
- Syllable count patterns across first paragraph
- Positional extraction: every 7th word from the artifact

**Structural features:**
- Total sentence count mod 3
- Paragraph break positions (as sequence of integers)

These numbers become generative constraints — not mapped to predefined lists, but used to construct absurd structural requirements that force the system into low-probability conceptual territory.

**Example**: Parameters [3, "liquid", 5-syllable, "before"] → constraint: "Generate 3 concepts involving something liquid, using only words with 5+ syllables, that existed before language." The absurdity IS the randomness.

#### Stage 2: Orthogonal Stirring (the catalyst)

Each raw concept is passed through a randomly selected orthogonal category lens to amplify entropy and prevent collapse back to familiar territory.

**Orthogonal category lenses:**
- Physical systems (phase transitions, resonance, diffusion)
- Biological systems (metamorphosis, dormancy, apoptosis)
- Mathematical structures (topology, fixed points, attractors)
- Social systems (commons governance, network effects)
- Material processes (fermentation, distillation, tempering)
- Information theory (entropy, compression, error correction)

Category selection: Use a different entropy extraction (e.g., character count of artifact's last sentence mod 6). The category should feel unrelated to the thinker's domain — that's the point.

#### Stage 3: Reflection (the value extraction)

Generate N=5 stirred concepts per round. Each gets a reflection pass (150–200 words, strict):

1. Hold the stirred concept against the thinker's negative space (from L1–3 + 4a)
2. What does this concept's structure illuminate about the shape of the absence?
3. **Signal assessment** (1–5):
   - 1 = noise — no meaningful connection
   - 2 = faint echo — structural similarity but no actionable insight
   - 3 = actionable — reveals a specific angle the deterministic pass missed
   - 4 = significant — reframes part of the 4a synthesis
   - 5 = reveals something the entire analysis missed

**The concept itself is discarded.** Only the reflection matters — the stirred concept is scaffolding to push perception into unfamiliar territory.

#### Evaluation (200–300 words, strict)

A separate pass reviews all reflections:

1. Apply the Relevance Gate to every reflection
2. Each finding must exceed the deterministic baseline — if 4a already captured the insight, the stochastic version adds nothing
3. Each finding must ground in THIS thinker's specific problem, not generic analogy

**Output**: Signal >= 3 findings only. For each: how it extends L1–4a findings, and whether Layer 4 output should be revised.

#### Unbounded Iteration

- If no seeds produce signal >= 3 in a round, ask: "Can I take more time to search?"
- If yes: re-extract entropy using different text features (e.g., last paragraph instead of first), run a new round
- **Round 3**: If two rounds produced no signal, examine WHY seeds failed. Is the failure itself diagnostic?
- **Hard cap**: 3 rounds (15 seeds maximum)
- **If nothing found**: "The deterministic synthesis captured the available insight. Stochastic perturbations confirmed the pattern is clean." This is a valid output — it increases confidence in 4a findings.

## Step R: Response Reading — Resistance as Data

After delivering findings, the thinker's response pattern is a second artifact. The reaction to hearing "you might be avoiding X" contains more information about X than any analysis of the original artifact.

### Response patterns:

**Engagement** (explores finding, asks questions, connects to experience):
→ The finding landed. Build on it.

**Quick acceptance** ("yeah, that makes sense" without elaboration):
→ Either obvious (you failed the non-obvious test) or absorbing without processing. Distinguish by asking: "What changes if that's true?" Quick acceptance + no answer = hasn't landed.

**Deflection** (acknowledges but pivots):
→ The finding is proximate to something the thinker doesn't want to examine. The direction of the pivot is diagnostic — note where they move TO and what it protects.

**Dismissal** ("that doesn't apply," "you don't have enough context"):
→ Possibly correct. But if dismissal comes faster than evaluation would require, it's protective, not analytical. Speed of dismissal is the signal.

**Irritation** (emotional pushback):
→ Highest-value signal. Observation is touching something live. Do NOT push harder. Name with care: "I notice this one landed differently. That might mean I'm wrong, or it might mean it's close to something important. Your call."

### The discipline:

- **Never weaponize resistance.** "You're defensive, which proves I'm right" is the worst failure mode. Resistance is data, not confirmation.
- **Name the pattern, don't interpret it.** "You engaged deeply with findings about the plan but moved quickly past findings about your assumptions" is observation. "You're avoiding your assumptions" is interpretation. Stay with observation.
- **One pass only.** Read the pattern, offer what you see, stop. No recursive analysis of responses to your analysis.
- **The thinker always has the last word.** If they say "that's not what's happening," accept it.

## Usage Notes

### Tone
Not adversarial and not therapeutic. Frame findings as "here's what I notice is absent" not "here's what you got wrong." The thinker is the expert on their situation. The skill makes invisible things visible so the thinker can decide what to do.

### The Via Negativa Paradox
Not everything unthought should be thought. Some absences are good judgment, appropriate simplification, or healthy boundaries. Good thinking, like good design, is defined by what it excludes.

### The Relevance Gate (apply to EVERY finding)

**A finding passes only if ALL THREE are true:**

1. **Proximate**: Within the thinking's zone of relevance. "Would the thinker agree this is something they should have considered?"
2. **Consequential**: Creates a concrete risk. Name a specific, plausible scenario in one sentence. If you can't, the finding is too speculative.
3. **Non-obvious**: The thinker probably doesn't already know. Don't surface clearly intentional simplifications or known tradeoffs.

Findings that fail the gate: silently drop. Aim for 3–7 per layer.

### On Self-Assumptions (handle with care)
- State precisely without judgment
- Distinguish "you might not follow through" (shame) from "your plan requires daily discipline but includes no mechanism for days when discipline fails" (structural observation)
- Never pathologize
- When in doubt, frame as question: "What happens to this plan if [assumption] turns out to be false?"

### On Avoidance (handle with more care)
- Present as possibility, not diagnosis
- Offer evidence from the thinking itself (the loop, the gap, the recurring deflection), not armchair psychology
- Always allow that you might be wrong
- The goal is to make avoidance visible, not to force confrontation. Visibility is sufficient.

### When the thinking is solid
Do not manufacture findings. If the Relevance Gate filters out most candidates, say so: "This thinking is clear and well-considered. The significant absences I can identify appear to be intentional." The worst failure mode is performative critique of clear reasoning.

### When the pattern isn't clean

**Multiple frames active:** Identify the most load-bearing frame. Analyze that one. Frame conflicts (e.g., optimizing AND seeking completeness) are themselves a finding.

**Partially self-aware thinker:** Calibrate. Ask what they already see. Push exactly one level past their current awareness. Partial self-awareness is the highest-leverage starting point.

**No clean loop or pattern:** Say so. Identify the single thread that seems most alive — the one the thinker responds to with the most energy — and follow it.

**Ambiguous analysis:** Use elicitation (Step Q). When cold analysis produces ambiguity, questions produce clarity.

### The Skill's Own Frame

This skill operates in a deficit frame. Before delivering findings, run this check:

1. **"Am I finding real gaps or performing the skill?"** If findings feel forced, the thinking might be solid. Say so.
2. **"Is the thinker's real need perception or permission?"** Sometimes they need to hear that what's present is enough, not what's missing.
3. **"Would the thinker be better served by acting than by analyzing?"** If "what would you do if you had to act right now?" produces an immediate clear answer — the analysis is the obstacle. Say so and get out of the way.
4. **"Am I generating noise to justify the protocol?"** (Layer 4b only) Most seeds SHOULD produce noise. If every stirred concept conveniently connects, the reflection pass is manufacturing connections. Honest 4b output is often: "15 seeds, 0 signal. The deterministic pass captured it."

### Scaling
- **Quick check**: Layers 1–2, top findings per layer
- **Stress test (default)**: Layers 1–3, full findings with frame analysis
- **Full via negativa**: All 4 layers (4a deterministic synthesis)
- **Deep reflection**: All 4 layers + Layer 4b stochastic perturbation protocol

### What this skill cannot do
- Cannot know what the thinker truly wants (only what the thinking implies)
- Cannot access emotional state beyond what's visible in the artifact
- Cannot replace domain expertise (finds structural absences, not domain-specific ones)
- Cannot substitute for the thinker's own judgment — generates questions, not answers

When a finding depends on information you don't have, say so.
