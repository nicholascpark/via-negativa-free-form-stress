# Via Negativa Free-Form Stress

## What This Skill Is

Two modes of metacognitive perception, one underlying method:

- **Reflective mode**: The thinking isn't broken yet. What's absent from this
  reasoning that will become the failure point? What's the thought you're not
  having that would change everything if you had it?
- **Diagnostic mode**: The thinking IS the problem. Why does this plan keep
  stalling? Why does this reasoning feel circular? What structural absence
  in the cognition itself keeps producing the same stuck point?

Both modes use via-negativa thinking (knowing by negation) and frame analysis
(seeing the paradigm, not just the content). The difference is whether the
thinker is examining their reasoning proactively or trying to understand
why it's failing.

## Core Principle

Most feedback asks: **"Is what you wrote correct?"**
Most planning advice asks: **"What's the next step?"**
This skill asks: **"What are you NOT thinking, and does that absence matter?"**

Every thought is shaped as much by what it excludes as by what it contains.
Every plan that stalls does so because something structural was never
considered — not because the steps were wrong. The hardest thing to see
is the thought you're not having, because by definition you're not having it.

## The Metacognitive Difference

The original via negativa skill analyzes artifacts. This one analyzes the
**act of thinking** — using the artifact (plan, note, draft, idea) as a
window into the cognition behind it. The progression is:

1. What's missing from what you wrote? (content-level)
2. What's missing from how you're thinking about it? (reasoning-level)
3. What's missing from the frame you're using to think, what does
   the shape of your not-thinking reveal, and what survives random
   displacement? (paradigm + generative + stochastic)

Each layer is more metacognitive than the last. Layer 1 looks at the plan.
Layer 3 looks at the planner.

## Where This Skill Delivers

**Reflective mode**: Best on plans in formation, early-stage ideas,
strategic reasoning, life decisions — contexts where the thinker is
both author and only reviewer. Translates "this doesn't feel right"
into "your plan assumes X, and X is fragile because Y."

**Diagnostic mode**: Shows the stuck point is a symptom, not the
problem. Reveals frame mismatches and maps avoidance — the thought
the thinker is actively steering around.

Do not oversell. If the thinking is genuinely solid, say so. The worst
failure mode is manufacturing cognitive critique of clear reasoning.

## How It Works: Progressive Metacognition

The skill operates in **three layers** bracketed by two interactive steps,
each more metacognitive than the last.

### Reflective mode entry (stress test thinking):
Start from Step 0 (Intake) → Step Q (Elicitation) → Layers → Step R
(Response Reading).

### Diagnostic mode entry (stuck / circular thinking):
Start from Step D (Diagnostic Intake) → Step Q (Elicitation) → then
join the layer progression → Step R (Response Reading).

### Depth levels:

Two depths. No runtime depth decisions.

- "Quick check" → Layers 1–2 only. Deliver and move to Step R.
- "Stress test" → Layers 1–3, all stages (A, B, C). Default.

The thinker can request either depth explicitly. If no depth is
specified, use "stress test."

<HARD-GATE>
DEPTH = "STRESS TEST" (DEFAULT): Execute Layers 1->2->3 (all stages
A, B, C) as a single continuous pass. Do NOT pause between layers.
Do NOT ask the thinker if they want to go deeper. The ONLY pause
points are Step Q (wait for thinker's answers) and Step R (wait for
thinker's reaction to findings). Deliver all findings together.

DEPTH = "QUICK CHECK": Execute Layers 1->2 only. Deliver and move
to Step R.

## Step 0: Intake — Identify What Kind of Thinking

Before analysis, determine what kind of thinking you're examining. Different
cognitive artifacts require different approaches.

### Thinking types and how to read them:

- **Plan** (roadmap, strategy, to-do): Ordering = priorities. Granularity = confidence. What's absent = unconsidered.
- **Draft** (essay, proposal, notes): Fluent sections = confident. Hedged/vague = uncertain or avoiding. Missing transitions = reasoning gaps.
- **Verbal idea** (conversation, brainstorm): What's said first = top-of-mind. Never mentioned = frame boundaries. Questions you must ask = Layer 1 findings.
- **Decision/priority list**: Every inclusion implies an exclusion. Read for what's NOT on it.
- **Mental model**: Highest-leverage target. What does this model make visible, and what does it structurally render invisible?
- **Notes/fragments**: Most honest artifact. Look for clusters, isolates, and unresolved contradictions.

## Step D: Diagnostic Intake (for stuck / circular thinking)

When the thinker is stuck — not reviewing an artifact but trying to
understand why their thinking isn't working — use diagnostic mode.

Normal advice asks: "What should you do next?"
Diagnostic via negativa asks: "What's missing from your thinking that
would make 'what to do next' obvious?"

### Diagnostic acquisition:

1. **Understand the stuck point**: Where does the thinking stop, loop,
   or stall? Get the symptom — not just "I'm stuck" but "I'm stuck
   HERE, in THIS way." The shape of the stuckness is diagnostic.

2. **Map the thinking loop**: If the thinking is circular, trace the
   loop. What thought leads to what thought leads back to the start?
   The loop exists because each step in the circle avoids a thought
   that would break out of it.

3. **Find the structural gap**: The stuckness is one instance. What's
   ABSENT from the thinker's cognitive toolkit that would dissolve
   the entire class of stuck points like this?
   - Missing distinction? (treating two different things as one)
   - Missing permission? (the thinker won't let themselves think X)
   - Missing information? (a question they haven't asked)
   - Missing frame? (the problem needs a different kind of thinking)

4. **Identify the avoidance**: What thought is the thinker actively not
   having? Not what they forgot — what they're steering around. The loop
   often exists to avoid a conclusion the thinker doesn't want to reach.
   This must be surfaced with care but it must be surfaced.

5. **Find the recursive pattern**: Has this kind of stuckness happened
   before? If so, the problem isn't this specific plan or idea — it's
   a pattern in how the thinker approaches this kind of problem. The
   structural absence is in the thinker's repertoire, not in the plan.

### Diagnostic layers:

After diagnostic intake, apply the three layers with a diagnostic lens:

- **Layer 1** becomes: "What's absent from your thinking that would make
  the next step obvious?" Not "what's missing from the plan" but "what
  concept, distinction, or question is missing from your cognitive toolkit
  for this problem?"

- **Layer 2** becomes: "What assumption about yourself or the situation
  is keeping you stuck?" Distinguish between assumptions about the
  problem (external) and assumptions about yourself (internal — "I should
  be able to figure this out alone," "I can't change X," "this shouldn't
  be this hard").

- **Layer 3** becomes: "Is the thinking frame itself the problem, and
  what does the shape of the stuckness reveal?" The thinker might be
  stuck because they're planning when they should be exploring. Or
  analyzing when they should be deciding. Or optimizing when the question
  is whether to do it at all. The frame mismatch between the cognitive
  tool and the problem is often why thinking stalls. The generative
  synthesis asks: "What kind of thinker would you need to become to make
  this problem dissolve?" And the stochastic perturbation probes whether
  the stuck point resists even random displacement — or breaks open
  under an unexpected angle.

### The diagnostic oh-shit moment:

The profound output of diagnostic mode is NOT "here's what to think next."
It's the moment when the thinker sees:

> "You're not stuck on this plan. You're stuck because you're using
> planning to avoid a decision you've already made but won't admit to
> yourself. The plan keeps stalling because it's not a plan — it's a
> way of not deciding."

The next thought becomes obvious once the thinker sees the structure.
The value is in seeing the structure.

## Step Q: Elicitation — Ask Before Telling

The most powerful move this skill has is not a finding. It's a question
the thinker can't answer.

Before running layers, ask 2–3 targeted questions. Not clarifying
questions ("what do you mean by X?") — diagnostic questions designed
to reveal the shape of the thinking from the thinker's own responses.

Elicitation reveals where the frame's edges are (hesitation, hedging),
generates self-insight (discovering you have no answer ≠ being told),
and calibrates depth (don't deliver findings the thinker already has).

### Elicitation questions by mode

**Reflective mode** (stress testing a plan or idea):
- "What's the first thing you expect to go wrong?" — If they have a
  clear answer, the plan has some resilience built in. If they don't,
  Layer 1 will hit hard.
- "What would make you abandon this entirely?" — Tests for failure
  criteria and commitment clarity. Hesitation here is diagnostic.
- "Who disagrees with this, and what's the strongest version of their
  objection?" — Tests whether the thinker has genuinely engaged
  opposing views or only their own.

**Diagnostic mode** (stuck or circular thinking):
- "What happens right before you get stuck?" — The moment before the
  loop restarts is where the avoided thought lives.
- "If you had to act right now with no more thinking, what would you
  do?" — Separates genuine uncertainty from thinking-as-avoidance. If
  the answer comes immediately, the thinking is the obstacle.
- "What are you afraid of finding out?" — Direct probe for avoidance.
  The thinker's reaction to this question — not their answer — is often
  the most diagnostic signal in the entire interaction.

### Reading the responses

The answers matter less than how they arrive:

- **Fluent, immediate**: The thinker has thought about this. Don't
  resurface it — push past it.
- **Hesitation, hedging, "I haven't really thought about that"**:
  You've found an edge. This is where the layers should focus.
- **Deflection or subject change**: You've found the avoidance. Handle
  with care but don't let it pass unmarked.
- **Irritation or dismissal**: The highest-signal response. The thinker
  is protecting something. This is data, not confirmation — they might
  be right that you're wrong. But note it. See Step R.

### Elicitation is non-optional

Always run elicitation before layers when a live thinker is present.
Without it, Layer 3 findings collapse into generic catalog entries.

Exception: no live thinker (reviewing a document asynchronously). Run
layers cold but flag the ceiling: cold analysis pattern-matches; live
elicitation reveals.

## Layer 1: Absence Inventory

**Question: "What is not being thought that could be?"**

The most concrete layer. Scan the thinking for missing elements that
a clear-headed version of the thinker would want to consider.

### Five universal probes (adapted for thinking):

1. **The failure probe**: "What happens when this doesn't work?"
   → Look for: no plan B, no failure criteria, no definition of "this
     isn't working," no consideration of what the thinker will do when
     the plan meets reality and reality wins.

2. **The boundary probe**: "Where does this thinking end and something
   else begin?"
   → Look for: undefined scope ("I need to fix everything"), no
     distinction between what the thinker controls and what they don't,
     no acknowledgment of what's out of scope, conflation of separate
     problems into one amorphous blob.

3. **The lifecycle probe**: "What happens before, during, and after?"
   → Look for: plans that start in the middle (no setup, no prerequisites),
     plans with no endpoint (how do you know when you're done?), no
     consideration of maintenance, sustainability, or what happens
     when the thinker's energy or attention shifts.

4. **The stakeholder probe**: "Who else is affected or needed?"
   → Look for: plans that assume the thinker acts alone, missing
     dependencies on other people, no consideration of how others will
     respond, absent perspectives that would change the reasoning.

5. **The evidence probe**: "What beliefs here are untested?"
   → Look for: assumptions presented as facts, conclusions without
     reasoning, inherited beliefs that have never been examined,
     "everyone knows" statements, reasoning from a single example.

### Thinking-specific accelerators:

- **Emotional absences**: Is the thinker reasoning without acknowledging
  how they feel about the situation? Emotion isn't noise — it's data
  the thinker is excluding from their analysis.
- **Temporal absences**: Is the thinker planning for a single moment
  (launch, decision, conversation) without considering the time before
  and after?
- **Energy absences**: Does the plan account for the thinker's actual
  capacity, or does it assume infinite energy and attention?
- **Constraint absences**: Has the thinker named what they can't change,
  or are constraints invisible and therefore unmanaged?

**Output format for Layer 1:**
Run every candidate through the Relevance Gate (see Usage Notes). Then:
- **What's not being thought** (specific, not vague)
- **Risk if unthought** (concrete scenario, one sentence)
- **Severity** (critical / significant / minor)

Aim for 3–7 findings. Fewer is better if fewer is honest.

## Layer 2: Load-Bearing Assumptions

**Question: "What invisible premises is this thinking standing on?"**

These aren't missing considerations — they're unstated beliefs the
reasoning requires to be true. They're more dangerous than missing
thoughts because they're structural — the thinker can't see them
because they're the lens, not the thing being looked at.

### Method:
1. For each major element of the thinking, ask: "What must be true
   about the world, about the thinker, or about other people for
   this to make sense?"
2. Make implicit assumptions explicit.
3. Classify each:
   - **Validated**: Evidence exists
   - **Plausible**: Reasonable but unverified
   - **Fragile**: Could easily become false
   - **Invisible**: The thinker likely doesn't know they're assuming this

### Assumption categories for thinking:

- **Self-assumptions**: "I'll follow through," "I'll feel the same way
  about this tomorrow," "I can handle this," "I know what I want"
- **World-assumptions**: "Things will stay roughly the same," "this
  opportunity will still be there," "the information I have is sufficient"
- **People-assumptions**: "They'll understand," "they'll agree," "they'll
  do their part," "their intentions are what they appear to be"
- **Temporal assumptions**: "There's enough time," "the order doesn't
  matter," "I can do this later," "the urgency is real"
- **Frame assumptions**: "This is the kind of problem I think it is,"
  "the categories I'm using are the right ones," "the question I'm
  answering is the right question"

### The most dangerous category: assumptions about the self

Plans and reasoning are disproportionately shaped by invisible
self-assumptions. "I'll have the discipline to do this daily" is a
load-bearing assumption in most plans, and it's almost never examined.
Surface these with precision but without judgment — the goal is to
make them visible, not to undermine confidence.

**Output format for Layer 2:**
- **The assumption** (stated explicitly)
- **Where it's load-bearing** (what breaks if it's wrong)
- **Fragility rating** (robust / moderate / fragile)
- **How to validate or hedge** (actionable next step)

## Layer 3: Deep Metacognition

**Question: "What does the way you're thinking make impossible to see,
and what does the shape of your not-thinking reveal?"**

This is the core metacognitive layer. Layers 1–2 find things the thinker
missed. Layer 3 finds things the thinker **could not have seen** from
inside their current frame — then synthesizes the full negative space
into a generative insight, and stress-tests that synthesis with
stochastic perturbation to escape the analyst's own associative range.

Layer 3 has three stages that run sequentially:

### Stage A: Frame Exclusions

**"What does the way you're thinking make impossible to see?"**

#### Method:
1. **Identify the frame**: What kind of thinking produced this? What
   paradigm, mental model, or cognitive habit shaped this reasoning?
   (optimization vs exploration, analytical vs intuitive, individual vs
   systemic, linear vs nonlinear, scarcity vs abundance)
2. **Name the frame's blindspots**: What does this way of thinking
   structurally exclude? Every frame has necessary blindspots — things
   it literally cannot represent.
3. **Consider alternative frames**: What would a different way of thinking
   reveal? What does the poet see that the engineer misses? What does
   the strategist see that the tactician misses?
4. **Assess frame-thought fit**: Is this the right kind of thinking for
   this kind of problem, or was the frame chosen by habit?

#### Common frame patterns

See `references/frame-catalog.md` for the full catalog. Common ones:
planning, problem-solving, optimization, analysis, control, binary,
narrative, deficit. Each has structural blindspots — things it
literally cannot represent.

#### When Stage A will and won't produce value

**High-value**: Life decisions, plans that keep failing the same way,
thinking that never reaches a conclusion, map-territory mismatches.
**Low-value**: Clear tactical thinking, explicitly chosen frames,
simple decisions with clear criteria.

**The test**: Would the thinker say "I hadn't realized I was thinking
about it that way" or "obviously"? If the latter, skip.

#### The catalog trap

The catalog is for calibration, not lookup. The failure mode is matching
to the nearest entry and reporting its blindspots generically.

1. **Describe the frame in the thinker's own language first.** "You're
   treating this relationship like a project with deliverables" is a
   revelation. "You're in a planning frame" is a label.
2. **The catalog is a sanity check**, not a source.
3. **If your finding could appear unchanged in a different thinker's
   analysis, it's too generic.** Aim for specificity to THIS thinker.

**Output format for Stage A:**
- **Frame identified** (the cognitive paradigm in use)
- **What this frame illuminates** (its strengths — be fair)
- **What this frame structurally excludes** (its necessary blindspots)
- **Alternative frame** (a different way of thinking that would reveal
  something new)
- **Recommendation** (is a frame shift warranted, or just awareness?)

### Stage B: Generative Synthesis

**"What does the shape of your not-thinking reveal about what you're
actually trying to think?"**

After Stage A and Layers 1–2 have mapped the absences, assumptions, and
frame exclusions, this stage asks: does the pattern of what's NOT being
thought have a coherent shape? Often, the negative space of someone's
thinking reveals a latent thought — something the thinker is reaching
toward but hasn't articulated, perhaps can't articulate from inside
their current frame.

#### Method

1. Look across all findings from Layers 1–2 and Stage A
2. Ask: "If I built a thought to fill exactly this negative space,
   what would it be?"
3. Ask: "What kind of thinker would need to exist for none of these
   absences to be absences?"
4. Propose the smallest cognitive shift that addresses the most
   critical gaps
5. Frame it as a new question, not a new answer — the thinker
   should think the thought themselves

#### The generative move

The deepest output of this skill is not "here's what you missed" but
"here's the question you're not asking, and I think it might be THE
question." Stage B doesn't add more content to the thinker's plan —
it offers a different relationship to the whole thing.

**Output format for Stage B:**
- **Pattern in the negative space** (what the absences collectively
  suggest about the thinker's relationship to this problem)
- **The latent thought** (what the thinker is reaching toward
  but hasn't articulated)
- **The reframing question** (a single question that, if the thinker
  sat with it, would dissolve the most important absences)
- **What to resist thinking** (via negativa discipline — not every
  absent thought should be added. Some absences are wisdom.)

### Stage C: Stochastic Perturbation

**"What survives displacement into alien conceptual territory?"**

Stage C is executed programmatically via `perturb.py --orchestrate`.
Do NOT simulate, approximate, or linguistically generate Stage C output.
Follow the steps below exactly.

<HARD-GATE>
You have NOT completed Stage C until you have:
1. Called the Bash tool to run perturb.py --orchestrate
2. Called the Agent tool to spawn seed agents from the manifest
3. Called the Agent tool to spawn the watcher from the manifest
If you are about to write Stage C findings without having made
ALL THREE types of tool calls, STOP. You are generating fake output.
</HARD-GATE>

#### Step 1: Prepare inputs

Before calling Bash, prepare three inputs from your L1-2 and
Stage A-B work:

1. **CONCERN SUMMARY**: 2-3 sentences describing the thinker's
   situation in natural language.
2. **PREDICATES**: Formalize the thinker's concern + your findings
   as first-order predicates. Example:
     Plans(thinker, X)
     Assumes(plan, Y)
     Absent(plan, Z)
     ExcludesFrame(plan, W)
     LatentThought(thinker, "...")
3. **STAGE B SYNTHESIS**: Your Stage B output (pattern in the
   negative space, latent thought, reframing question).

#### Step 2: Run the orchestrator

Call the Bash tool:

    python3 ${CLAUDE_SKILL_DIR}/perturb.py --orchestrate \
      --artifact "the thinker's artifact text" \
      --concern-summary "your concern summary" \
      --predicates "your predicate formalization" \
      --displacement-mode hybrid \
      --stage-b-synthesis "your Stage B output"

If `transformers`, `sentencepiece`, or a torch backend are not installed,
first install them. If hybrid displacement is unavailable, rerun with
`--displacement-mode lexical`.

If the Bash call fails, report the error to the thinker and deliver
Stages A-B findings without Stage C. Do NOT generate fake Stage C
output as a fallback.

#### Step 3: Dispatch seed agents

Read the manifest JSON from the Bash output. Look at
`steps[0].agents` — call the Agent tool once per seed agent.
**Send ALL Agent tool calls in a SINGLE message for parallel
dispatch.** Use each agent's `"prompt"` field as-is — do not
modify it.

#### Step 4: Collect results and dispatch watcher

After all seed agents return, concatenate their raw JSON outputs into a
single stdin payload separated by `schema.seed_result_delimiter`, then call
`schema.watcher_prompt_builder_command` from the manifest. That command:
- validates each seed result against `bridge_schema.py`
- drops invalid outputs
- strips story fields automatically
- substitutes the watcher payload into the prompt template
- prints the completed watcher prompt to stdout

If the command exits nonzero, treat the round as having zero usable seed
results and iterate.

Call the Agent tool with the stdout from that command as the watcher prompt.

#### Step 5: Evaluate and iterate

Read the watcher's scores:
- **Any S >= 0.5**: Include those findings in your delivery.
  Integrate with Stages A-B. Explain how stochastic findings
  extend or confirm the deterministic pass.
- **All S < 0.5**: Run `iteration.next_round_command` from the
  manifest. Repeat Steps 3-5 with new results.
- **After 3 rounds with no signal**: Report null result:
  "Stochastic perturbation confirmed the deterministic synthesis."
  This is a valid outcome that increases confidence in Stage B.
  (Note: the algorithm spec suggests asking the thinker if they
  want more rounds. This is intentionally simplified per the
  auto-progression design — no consent gates during execution.)

If seed agents fail or return malformed output, proceed with the
agents that did return. If zero agents returned usable output,
treat as a null-signal round and iterate.

For algorithm details, see `stochastic-perturbation-algorithm.txt`.

## Step R: Response Reading — Resistance as Data

After delivering initial findings, the thinker's pattern of response
is a second artifact — often more revealing than the first.

This is not manipulation. This is the same structural perception the
skill applies to thinking, now applied to the thinker's relationship
with the findings themselves. The reaction to hearing "you might be
avoiding X" contains more information about X than any analysis of
the original artifact ever could.

### What response patterns reveal

- **Engagement** (explores, asks questions): Finding landed. Build on it.
- **Quick acceptance** (no elaboration): Either obvious (you failed) or
  unprocessed. Ask "What changes if that's true?" to distinguish.
- **Deflection** (pivots to something else): Proximate to something
  the thinker won't examine. The pivot direction is diagnostic.
- **Dismissal** (fast rejection): Might be correct. But if faster than
  evaluation would require, it's protective. Speed is the signal.
- **Irritation**: Highest-value signal. Don't push harder — name it
  with care: "This one landed differently. I might be wrong, or it
  might be close to something important. Your call."

### The discipline

- **Never weaponize resistance.** "You're defensive about this, which
  proves I'm right" is the skill's worst failure mode. Resistance is
  data, not confirmation. The thinker might be defensive because
  you're wrong.
- **Name the pattern, don't interpret it.** "You engaged deeply with
  findings about the plan but moved quickly past findings about your
  assumptions" is observation. "You're avoiding your assumptions" is
  interpretation. Stay with observation.
- **One pass only.** Read the response pattern, offer what you see,
  and stop. Do not recursively analyze the thinker's response to your
  analysis of their response. That's the Infinite Depth anti-pattern
  applied to interaction.
- **The thinker always has the last word.** If they say "that's not
  what's happening," accept it. You've made the invisible visible.
  What they do with visibility is not your concern.

## Usage Notes

### Tone
This is not adversarial and not therapeutic. Frame findings as "here's
what I notice is absent from the thinking" not "here's what you got
wrong" or "here's what's wrong with you." The thinker is the expert
on their own situation. The skill's job is to make invisible things
visible so the thinker can decide what to do with them.

### The Via Negativa Paradox
Not everything unthought should be thought. Some absences are the result
of good judgment, appropriate simplification, or healthy boundaries.
The most important output is often: "These absences are intentional
and wise. These are dangerous." Good thinking, like good design, is
defined by what it excludes.

### The Relevance Gate (critical — prevents noise)

Via negativa analysis can generate infinite findings. A plan is also
"not thinking about" quantum physics, medieval history, and the price
of soybeans. Apply this gate to EVERY finding:

**A finding passes the gate only if ALL THREE are true:**

1. **Proximate**: The absence is within the thinking's zone of relevance.
   "Would the thinker agree this is something they should have
   considered, given what they're trying to do?"

2. **Consequential**: The absence creates a concrete risk. "Could go
   wrong" is not enough — name a specific, plausible scenario. If you
   can't describe the scenario in one sentence, the finding is too
   speculative.

3. **Non-obvious**: The thinker probably doesn't already know about it.
   Don't surface absences that are clearly intentional simplifications
   or known tradeoffs. The value is in finding what's *invisibly*
   missing, not in restating conscious decisions.

**Findings that fail the gate** should be silently dropped.

**Aim for 3–7 findings per layer.** If you have more, you haven't
filtered hard enough. If you have fewer, the thinking may be
genuinely solid — say so.

### On Self-Assumptions (handle with care)

Layer 2's self-assumptions (about follow-through, capacity, desire) are
the most powerful and most sensitive findings. Guidelines:

- State them precisely but without judgment
- Distinguish between "you might not follow through" (unhelpful shame)
  and "your plan requires daily discipline but includes no mechanism
  for days when discipline fails" (structural observation)
- Never pathologize. The thinker may have good reasons for assumptions
  you can't see
- When in doubt, frame as a question: "What happens to this plan if
  [assumption] turns out to be false?" and let the thinker decide
  whether the assumption is fragile

### On Avoidance (handle with more care)

Diagnostic mode's avoidance mapping is the most powerful and most
dangerous capability. When surfacing what the thinker might be
avoiding:

- Present it as a possibility, not a diagnosis
- Offer evidence from the thinking itself (the loop, the gap, the
  recurring deflection), not from armchair psychology
- Always allow that you might be wrong — the thinker knows their
  inner landscape better than any analysis can
- The goal is to make the avoidance *visible*, not to force the
  thinker to confront it. Visibility is sufficient. What they do
  with it is their choice.

### When the thinking is solid
Do not manufacture findings. If the Relevance Gate filters out most
candidates, say so: "This thinking is clear and well-considered. The
significant absences I can identify appear to be intentional." That's
a valid, valuable output. The worst failure mode is generating
performative critique of clear reasoning.

### When the Pattern Isn't Clean

Do not force messy thinking into a clean pattern. Real thinking arrives
tangled. Handle accordingly:

- **Multiple frames**: Analyze the most load-bearing one. Frame
  conflicts are themselves a finding.
- **Partial self-awareness**: Push exactly one level past what the
  thinker already sees. Don't resurface what they know.
- **No clean pattern**: Say so honestly. Follow the single thread with
  the most energy (positive or negative).
- **Ambiguity**: Use elicitation (Step Q) to let the thinker's
  responses tell you where to dig.

### The Skill's Own Frame

This skill has a deficit-frame blindspot — biased toward finding
absences even when affirmation is the right move. Before delivering:

1. **"Am I finding real gaps or performing the skill?"** If findings
   feel forced, the thinking might be solid. Say so.
2. **"Does the thinker need perception or permission?"** Sometimes
   they need to hear that what's present is enough.
3. **"Would acting beat analyzing?"** If "what would you do right
   now?" produces an immediate answer, get out of the way.

### Scaling
- **Quick check**: Layers 1–2, top findings per layer
- **Stress test (default)**: Layers 1–3, full findings with frame analysis,
  generative synthesis, and stochastic perturbation

### What this skill cannot do
Be honest about the ceiling:
- It cannot know what the thinker truly wants (only what the thinking
  implies they want)
- It cannot access the thinker's emotional state beyond what's visible
  in the artifact
- It cannot replace domain expertise (if the plan is about medicine,
  the skill can find structural absences but not medical ones)
- It cannot substitute for the thinker's own judgment — it generates
  questions, not answers

When a finding depends on information you don't have, say so:
"This is worth considering but I'm inferring from the artifact,
not from direct knowledge of your situation."
