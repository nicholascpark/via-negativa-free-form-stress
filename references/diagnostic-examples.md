# Diagnostic Examples (Stuck / Circular Thinking)

## Worked Example: The Plan That Keeps Restarting

### The Symptom
The thinker has been "planning to write a book" for two years. They have
14 different outlines, three chapter drafts, and a folder called "book
research" with 200+ bookmarks. They describe themselves as "still figuring
out the right approach."

### Step D: Diagnostic Intake

**The stuck point**: The thinker restarts the plan whenever the current
approach feels imperfect. Outline v1 was too broad. v2 was too narrow.
v3 didn't have the right tone. Each restart is accompanied by genuine
insight about why the previous approach was flawed. The thinking is
getting better. The book is not getting written.

**The thinking loop**:
Plan → Start → Encounter imperfection → Analyze flaw → Gain insight →
Restart with improved plan → (repeat)

**The structural gap**: The thinker has no concept of "good enough to
continue." Every flaw is treated as a reason to restart rather than
a problem to solve within the current attempt. The loop continues
because each restart feels like progress (better plan!) while actually
resetting to zero.

### Layer 1: What's absent that would dissolve this?

| Missing | What It Would Dissolve | Severity |
|---------|----------------------|----------|
| No distinction between "wrong approach" and "uncomfortable stage of the right approach" | The thinker can't tell whether the discomfort at draft 3 means the approach is wrong or means writing is hard; they default to "wrong approach" because restarting relieves the discomfort | Critical |
| No definition of "done enough to continue" | Without a threshold for acceptable imperfection, the thinker's quality bar rises with every restart — they know more, so they demand more, so nothing qualifies | Critical |
| No time-boxed commitment mechanism | The thinker can abandon an approach at any moment of doubt; there's no structural commitment to stay with discomfort long enough to learn whether it's signal or noise | Significant |

### Layer 2: The assumption that keeps the loop running

**"The right approach will feel right"** (Invisible, load-bearing)
- The thinker abandons each approach when it stops feeling right. The
  assumption is that the correct approach will be recognizable by its
  felt sense of correctness. But writing a book involves long stretches
  of uncertainty, confusion, and dissatisfaction with the work — this
  is normal, not diagnostic. The assumption makes every normal writing
  difficulty into evidence that the approach is wrong.
- Where it's load-bearing: It's the decision rule for every restart.
  Remove this assumption and the thinker would have to develop a
  different criterion for when to restart vs. when to push through.

**"Planning is a prerequisite for writing"** (Invisible)
- 14 outlines suggests the thinker treats planning and writing as
  sequential: plan → then write. But for many writers, writing IS
  planning — you discover the book by writing it, not by outlining it.
  The assumption that the outline must be right before writing starts
  creates an infinite prerequisite.

### Layer 3: The thinking frame is the problem

**Frame**: The thinker is in **optimization mode** — trying to find the
best approach before committing.

**What this frame illuminates**: Genuine improvements in understanding
(each outline IS better than the last).

**What this frame structurally excludes**: The possibility that the
"best approach" doesn't exist in advance — it can only be discovered
through sustained imperfect execution. Optimization mode is appropriate
for choosing between known options. It's inappropriate for creative
work where the options are generated by the work itself.

**The frame mismatch**: The thinker is applying an engineering mindset
(design before building) to a creative process (discover through
building). Both mindsets are valid. This one doesn't fit this problem.

**Alternative frame**: Exploration mode — "I'm going to write badly for
60 days and see what emerges. The writing IS the research."

### Layer 4: The generative move

**Pattern in the negative space**: Every absence points to the same
thing — the thinker has optimized the meta-process (planning the book)
to avoid the object-process (writing the book). The planning has become
the activity. The book is the excuse for the planning.

**The latent thought**: "What if I'm not planning a book? What if I'm
a person who enjoys thinking about writing more than writing, and
that's actually fine?"

**The reframing question**: "If the book were magically finished
tomorrow, what would you do? If the answer is 'start planning
another book,' the book isn't the point."

**What to resist thinking**: Don't make a better plan. Don't outline
v15. The pattern is clear: more planning = more delay. The via negativa
intervention is to REMOVE the planning phase, not improve it. Write
for 30 minutes. Then decide if you want to write for 30 more.

### The diagnostic oh-shit moment

> "You're not stuck on writing a book. You've built a perfect machine
> for not writing a book that feels exactly like writing a book. Each
> restart produces a better plan and zero pages. The 14 outlines aren't
> evidence of progress — they're the product of the loop. The question
> isn't 'which outline is right?' The question is 'what happens if you
> write without one?'"

## Worked Example: The Decision That Won't Resolve

### The Symptom
The thinker has been deciding whether to move cities for six months.
Pro/con lists. Spreadsheets comparing cost of living. Conversations
with friends in both cities. Research into neighborhoods, job markets,
social scenes. They describe themselves as "trying to make a data-driven
decision" but the decision never arrives.

### Step D: Diagnostic Intake

**The thinking loop**:
Research City A → Find appealing aspects → Research City B → Find
appealing aspects → Compare → Inconclusive → Research more →
(repeat with finer-grained data)

**The structural gap**: More information is not producing more clarity.
Each round of research adds data without changing the balance. The
thinker interprets this as "I need more data" when the actual signal
is "more data won't resolve this because the decision isn't about data."

### Layer 1: What's absent?

| Missing | What It Would Dissolve | Severity |
|---------|----------------------|----------|
| No decision criteria defined in advance | The thinker evaluates options without having decided what matters most; every new data point can shift the framing because the frame was never fixed | Critical |
| No acknowledgment that the decision involves loss | Every framing is about which option is "better"; no engagement with the fact that choosing one means grieving the other | Critical |
| No time limit on deciding | The decision has no deadline, so research can continue indefinitely; there's no structural forcing function | Significant |

### Layer 2: The assumptions

**"The right choice will be clearly right"** (Invisible, load-bearing)
- The thinker keeps researching because they believe enough data will
  make one option obviously superior. But the data keeps coming back
  balanced because the options ARE balanced. The assumption that
  "right" means "clearly superior" prevents the thinker from making
  a decision between two genuinely good options.
- Remove this assumption: the thinker would need to accept that this
  is a decision between two good-enough options, and the job is to
  choose, not to discover which is "right."

**"I can decide without losing anything"** (Invisible)
- The research phase is comfortable because it preserves both options.
  Deciding means losing one. The thinker is treating the decision as
  an optimization problem when it's actually a grief problem — they
  need to mourn the path not taken before they can commit to the path
  they choose.

### Layer 3: Frame mismatch

**Frame**: Decision analysis — rational evaluation of options against
criteria.

**Excludes**: The possibility that this decision can't be made
analytically because the variables that matter most (will I be happy?
will I make new friends? will it feel like home?) are not knowable
in advance. They can only be discovered by living there.

**The frame mismatch**: The thinker is using an analytical tool for an
experiential question. No amount of research about a city tells you
how it will feel to live there on a random Tuesday in February. The
analytical frame makes the thinker feel responsible for predicting the
unpredictable, which makes the decision feel higher-stakes than it is.

**Alternative frame**: Reversibility assessment — "Is this decision
reversible? If I move and hate it, can I move back?" If yes, the
stakes are much lower than the analytical frame implies, and the
decision becomes "which experiment do I want to run first?"

### Layer 4: The generative move

**The latent thought**: "Both options are fine. The thing I'm afraid of
isn't choosing wrong — it's choosing at all, because choosing means
the period of possibility is over."

**The reframing question**: "What would you do if both cities were
equally good and you knew it? What would the decision be about then?"

**What to resist thinking**: Don't make a better comparison framework.
Don't research more. The via negativa intervention is to REMOVE the
analytical apparatus and sit with the question: "Which life do I want
to try?"

## Worked Example: The Recurring Failure Pattern

### The Symptom
The thinker notices they keep starting exercise routines and stopping
after 2–3 weeks. They've tried: gym membership, home workouts, running,
yoga, a personal trainer. Each time they plan carefully, start
enthusiastically, and quit when they miss a few days. They want help
making a plan that "actually sticks."

### Step D: Diagnostic Intake

**The thinking loop**:
Choose new approach → Plan carefully → Start with enthusiasm →
Miss 2–3 days → Feel guilty → Interpret guilt as failure →
Abandon approach → Wait → Choose new approach → (repeat)

**The structural gap**: The thinker treats missing a day as failure
rather than as a normal part of any ongoing practice. The plan has
no mechanism for re-entry after interruption. It's binary: on track
or failed.

### Layer 1: What's absent?

| Missing | What It Would Dissolve | Severity |
|---------|----------------------|----------|
| No concept of "returning" as distinct from "restarting" | When the thinker misses days, the only option they see is to quit and start a new plan later; there's no cognitive pathway for "I missed three days and I'm going back tomorrow" | Critical |
| No minimum viable version | The plan is always the full version (5 days/week, 45 minutes, full routine); there's no "10-minute walk counts" fallback for low-energy days | Significant |
| No separation between the routine and the thinker's identity | Missing days triggers shame because the thinker interprets it as evidence about who they are ("I'm not a disciplined person") rather than as information about the plan ("this plan doesn't survive interruption") | Significant |

### Layer 2: The assumption

**"A plan that sticks requires perfect adherence"** (Invisible, deeply
load-bearing)
- The thinker's entire request — "make a plan that sticks" — assumes
  the problem is the plan. But 6+ different approaches have all failed
  in the same way at the same point. The constant is not the plan —
  it's the thinker's response to interruption. The plan doesn't need
  to be stickier. The thinker needs a different relationship to
  imperfect adherence.

### Layer 3: Frame mismatch

**Frame**: Achievement/compliance — exercise is a program to follow,
success is following it, failure is not following it.

**Excludes**: Exercise as practice — an ongoing relationship with
movement that has good weeks and bad weeks, that bends without
breaking, that defines success as "still doing it after a year"
regardless of how many days were missed.

The compliance frame guarantees failure because any real human life
will produce interruptions. The frame defines interruption as failure,
so failure is inevitable. The thinker then blames themselves or the
plan, when the actual cause is the frame.

### Layer 4: The generative move

**The latent thought**: The thinker doesn't need a new plan. They need
a new definition of success. "I exercised 3 out of 7 days this week"
needs to feel like success, not failure. The current frame makes
anything less than 100% adherence feel like 0%.

**The reframing question**: "What if you could never fail at this?
What if 'doing it badly and inconsistently for years' IS the goal?"

**What to resist thinking**: Don't design a more resilient plan.
Don't add accountability mechanisms, habit trackers, or reward systems.
These are all built on the compliance frame and will fail the same way.
The intervention is in the frame, not the plan.

### The diagnostic oh-shit moment

> "You've tried six different exercise plans and they've all failed in
> the same way — not because the plans were wrong but because your
> definition of success guarantees failure. You define success as
> unbroken adherence. Life breaks adherence. So you 'fail,' feel shame,
> quit, and start over with a 'better' plan that will also break. The
> plan is not the variable. Your relationship to imperfection is the
> variable. The question isn't 'how do I make a plan that sticks?' It's
> 'how do I keep going after I miss a day?'"

## Quick Diagnostic Questions for Any Stuck Thinking

1. **"What happens right before you get stuck?"**
   (The moment before the loop restarts is where the avoided thought lives)

2. **"Have you been stuck this way before, on something else?"**
   (If yes, the problem isn't this specific situation — it's a pattern)

3. **"What would you do if you couldn't think about this anymore and
   had to act right now?"**
   (Separates genuine uncertainty from productive procrastination)

4. **"What are you afraid of finding out?"**
   (The avoided thought is often an answer the thinker doesn't want)

5. **"Is the thinking helping, or has the thinking become the activity?"**
   (The moment thinking replaces doing, the thinking is the obstacle)
