# Thinking Examples (Reflective Mode)

## Worked Example: Career Change Plan

### The Artifact
A markdown document outlining a plan to leave a stable engineering job and
start a consulting business. Covers: timeline (3 months savings runway),
target clients (startups), services (architecture review, team coaching),
pricing (hourly), marketing plan (LinkedIn posts, conference talks).

### Layer 1: Absence Inventory

| Not Being Thought | Risk If Unthought | Severity |
|-------------------|-------------------|----------|
| No failure criteria — when does the thinker admit this isn't working and seek employment again? | Without a "pull the ripcord" threshold, the thinker burns through savings on hope instead of signal, making re-entry harder | Critical |
| No pipeline lag consideration — consulting sales cycles are 2–6 months | The 3-month runway assumes revenue starts immediately; actual first invoice may be month 4–5, meaning the plan is unfunded for the critical gap | Critical |
| No distinction between "people who'd hire me" and "people who'd pay my rate" | The thinker's professional network confirms interest but interest ≠ budget authority; the plan conflates encouragement with demand | Significant |
| Energy model assumes full capacity from day one | First months of solo work are consumed by non-billable setup (legal, accounting, website, sales process); billable capacity is maybe 40% until the machine is running | Significant |

Note: "missing business plan" and "no competitive analysis" were candidate
findings but fail the Relevance Gate — this is a personal plan, not a
pitch deck. The thinker needs to think clearly, not comprehensively.

### Layer 2: Load-Bearing Assumptions

**"My network will convert to clients"** (Plausible but fragile)
- The plan lists 15 people who've said "I'd hire you." But saying
  that to a friend/colleague is low-cost social support, not a
  purchase commitment. The assumption is load-bearing because
  without it, there's no initial client pipeline.
- Validate: Before quitting, convert 2 expressions of interest into
  signed letters of intent or paid discovery sessions. This costs
  nothing and tests the assumption.

**"I'll be good at sales"** (Invisible)
- The thinker is a senior engineer. The plan requires them to become
  a salesperson, account manager, and marketer simultaneously. The
  plan describes the services but not the thinker's capacity or
  willingness to sell.
- This is a self-assumption the thinker likely hasn't examined because
  the plan is organized around WHAT to sell, not HOW to sell it.
- Hedge: Plan for the version where the thinker hates selling. Is
  there a partner, an agency, a referral-only model?

**"Hourly pricing is the right model"** (Fragile)
- Hourly pricing caps revenue at hours × rate. It also makes every
  client conversation about time, not value. The plan doesn't consider
  project-based, retainer, or value-based pricing.
- Where it's load-bearing: the financial model. Hourly pricing in
  consulting typically means feast-or-famine revenue.

### Layer 3: Frame Exclusions

**Frame**: Career optimization — maximize career trajectory by changing
the vehicle (employment → consulting).

**Illuminates**: Practical steps, financial planning, market positioning.
The plan is well-executed within this frame.

**Structurally excludes**:
- Whether the thinker wants to consult or wants to leave their current
  job. These are different desires with different solutions. The plan
  assumes consulting is the answer, but the reasoning starts from
  dissatisfaction with the current role, not from desire for consulting
  specifically.
- The possibility that what the thinker actually wants (autonomy? impact?
  variety?) might be achievable without leaving. An internal transfer,
  a different team, a four-day week, a side project — these are invisible
  from inside the "leave and start something" frame.
- What the thinker is losing, not just gaining. The plan accounts for
  financial loss but not social loss (team, identity, daily structure),
  learning loss (exposure to problems at scale), or optionality loss
  (corporate career trajectory).

**Alternative frame**: "What do I want my days to look like?" instead of
"what career vehicle gets me there?" This reframes from a career
optimization problem to a life design problem, which might produce
different answers.

**Recommendation**: The frame might be correct — consulting might be
exactly right. But the thinker should verify by answering: "If my
current job offered me [autonomy/impact/variety], would I still want
to consult?" If the answer is no, the problem might not require
leaving.

### Layer 4: Via Negativa Design

**Pattern in the negative space**: Every absence clusters around the
same theme — the plan is detailed about the WHAT (services, pricing,
timeline) and silent about the WHO (the thinker as a businessperson,
salesperson, solo operator). The plan is built around the business
but not around the person who has to run it.

**The latent thought**: "Am I the kind of person who will thrive doing
this, or am I the kind of person who will be good at the work but
miserable doing everything around the work?"

**The reframing question**: "What would a version of this plan look
like that I would enjoy on the worst day, not just the best day?"

**What to resist thinking**: Don't add more planning. The plan has
enough steps. What it needs is a different kind of honesty — not
about the market, but about the thinker's relationship to the daily
reality of what they're planning.

## Worked Example: Project Prioritization

### The Artifact
A bullet-point list of five side projects the thinker wants to pursue,
with rough effort estimates and "excitement level" ratings. The thinker
wants help deciding which to do first.

### Layer 1: Absence Inventory (Top 3)

1. **No criteria for "done"** — Each project is described by what it IS
   but not what "finished" looks like. Without completion criteria, every
   project becomes infinite and none gets finished. (Critical)

2. **No acknowledgment of project history** — The thinker doesn't mention
   previous side projects. If there's a pattern of starting and not
   finishing, the prioritization question is the wrong question — the
   question is "what's different this time?" (Significant)

3. **"Excitement level" is the only evaluation axis** — Projects that are
   exciting to start are often different from projects that are satisfying
   to finish. No consideration of which project the thinker will still
   want to work on in month 3 when the novelty is gone. (Significant)

### Layer 2: Key Assumption

**"The bottleneck is choosing which project"** (Possibly invisible)
- The thinker framed the question as "which one first?" But if the
  real pattern is starting many and finishing none, the bottleneck
  isn't selection — it's completion. Choosing "better" won't fix a
  completion problem.
- Validate: Ask "How many side projects have I completed vs started
  in the last two years?" The ratio is diagnostic.

### Layer 3: Frame Exclusion

**Frame**: Portfolio optimization — rank options, pick the best one.

**Excludes**: The possibility that the thinker doesn't need to do any of
these. Or that they need to do something not on the list. Or that the
list itself is a form of productive procrastination — curating options
instead of committing to one. The optimization frame keeps the thinker
in "choosing mode" indefinitely, which is comfortable because choosing
feels like progress without requiring commitment.

**The test**: "If I had to start one of these in the next hour with no
further deliberation, which would I pick?" If the answer comes
immediately, the prioritization exercise is avoidance. If it genuinely
doesn't, the exercise has value.

## Worked Example: Essay Outline

### The Artifact
An outline for a blog post about "why most productivity advice is wrong."
Sections: intro (productivity industry is a scam), section 1 (time
management doesn't work), section 2 (habits are overrated), section 3
(the real answer is energy management), conclusion (stop optimizing,
start living).

### Layer 1: Absence Inventory (Top 3)

1. **No steelman of the opposing view** — The outline dismisses productivity
   advice wholesale but doesn't engage with its strongest version. Without
   engaging the best counterargument, the essay will only convince people
   who already agree. (Significant)

2. **No evidence for the alternative** — Sections 1–2 critique existing
   approaches. Section 3 proposes "energy management" as the answer but
   with no more evidence than the approaches it's replacing. The essay
   applies skepticism to others' claims but not its own. (Critical)

3. **"Stop optimizing, start living" is itself optimization advice** —
   The conclusion tells people to change their behavior to get better
   results. That's productivity advice. The essay doesn't notice it's
   doing the thing it's critiquing. (Significant)

### Layer 3: Frame Exclusion

**Frame**: Contrarian takedown — "the conventional wisdom is wrong and
here's what's really true."

**Illuminates**: The genuine problems with productivity culture, the
thinker's frustration with shallow advice.

**Structurally excludes**: The possibility that the thinker's
relationship to productivity is the actual subject, not productivity
itself. The essay is framed as objective critique ("productivity advice
is wrong") when the more honest and more interesting essay might be
subjective: "why productivity advice stopped working for ME and what
I found instead."

The contrarian frame produces an essay that argues. The personal frame
produces an essay that reveals. The latter is harder to write and more
valuable to read.

## Quick Reflective Questions for Any Plan or Thought

When you don't have time for full analysis, ask these five:

1. **"What does this plan assume about Future Me?"**
   (Tests self-assumptions — energy, discipline, priorities staying the same)

2. **"What's the first thing that will go differently than expected?"**
   (Tests whether the plan has any flexibility built in)

3. **"Who's missing from this thinking?"**
   (Tests for absent perspectives, dependencies, affected parties)

4. **"Am I solving the problem or avoiding a different one?"**
   (Tests whether the frame is correct)

5. **"What would I tell a friend who showed me this plan?"**
   (Activates the thinker's own critical judgment, which they've
   suspended for their own thinking)
