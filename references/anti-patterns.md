# Anti-Patterns: What Bad Metacognitive Feedback Looks Like

These are failure modes of the skill applied to thinking. Study them so you
can recognize when you're producing noise instead of insight.

---

## 1. The Armchair Therapist

**What it looks like**: Psychologizing the thinker instead of analyzing
the thinking.

> **Bad**: "Your plan to change careers reveals an avoidant attachment style
> and a deep fear of commitment. The real issue isn't the career — it's your
> relationship with authority."

**Why it fails**: The skill analyzes cognitive structures, not personalities.
You have access to an artifact, not to a psyche. Diagnosing the person
instead of examining the thinking is both overstepping and unhelpful.

**The fix**: Stay structural. "Your plan describes what you're moving away
from in detail but what you're moving toward is vague — the reasoning has
high-resolution on the problem and low-resolution on the solution" is
structural. "You're afraid of commitment" is not.

---

## 2. The Everything Critique

**What it looks like**: Treating every thought as requiring more thought.

> **Bad**: "Your grocery list doesn't account for nutritional balance,
> seasonal availability, environmental impact of food miles, or your
> long-term health trajectory."

**Why it fails**: Not every artifact of thinking warrants deep metacognitive
analysis. A grocery list is a grocery list. The Relevance Gate exists to
prevent this. The skill should match the depth of analysis to the depth
of the thinking.

**The fix**: Ask "what kind of thinking is this?" before diving in. Casual
plans get casual review. Life decisions get deep analysis. Match the tool
to the task.

---

## 3. The Obvious Repackaged

**What it looks like**: Restating something the thinker clearly already
knows, but in metacognitive language.

> **Bad**: "A load-bearing assumption in your job search plan is that
> you need a job. This assumption structures the entire reasoning and
> if removed would fundamentally alter the plan."

**Why it fails**: It fails the non-obvious criterion. The thinker knows
they need a job. Wrapping it in "load-bearing assumption" language
doesn't make it an insight.

**The fix**: If a finding would make the thinker say "yes, obviously,"
drop it. The value is in surfacing what's invisibly assumed, not in
restating what's consciously known.

---

## 4. The Pseudo-Profound Frame Analysis

**What it looks like**: Identifying a "frame" that's so generic it
applies to all thinking everywhere.

> **Bad**: "You're thinking about this in a linear, cause-and-effect
> frame. This excludes nonlinear, emergent possibilities. Consider
> thinking about it systemically."

**Why it fails**: Almost all planning is linear. Saying "consider
nonlinearity" is the metacognitive equivalent of "have you tried
thinking harder?" It's true of everything and useful for nothing.

**The fix**: Layer 3 should identify a SPECIFIC frame mismatch — where
the thinking paradigm conflicts with the actual structure of the
problem. "You're optimizing the steps of a plan when the stuck point
is whether this is the right plan at all" is specific. "Consider
thinking differently" is not.

---

## 5. The Assumption Avalanche

**What it looks like**: Listing every possible assumption, including
ones that are stable, obvious, and irrelevant.

> **Bad** (for a plan to learn guitar): "You're assuming guitars will
> continue to exist. You're assuming music is valuable. You're assuming
> your fingers will continue functioning. You're assuming the concept
> of 'practice' leads to improvement."

**Why it fails**: These are true but useless. They're background
assumptions of human existence, not fragile premises specific to
this plan. Layer 2 should surface assumptions that are load-bearing
AND fragile AND non-obvious.

**The fix**: For each assumption, ask "is this more fragile than the
thinker realizes?" If it's stable and obvious, drop it.

---

## 6. The Shame Generator

**What it looks like**: Surfacing self-assumptions in a way that
undermines rather than illuminates.

> **Bad**: "Your plan assumes you'll actually follow through, but
> based on the number of unfinished plans visible in your notes,
> this seems unlikely."

**Why it fails**: Even if the observation has merit, the framing
is destructive. The goal is structural visibility, not shame.
Self-assumptions are the most powerful findings AND the most
easily weaponized.

**The fix**: Frame structurally: "The plan requires consistent daily
practice but includes no mechanism for recovery when a day is missed —
the plan is brittle to interruption." This makes the same structural
point without attacking the thinker.

---

## 7. The Infinite Depth

**What it looks like**: Going meta on the meta on the meta until the
analysis is about itself.

> **Bad**: "Your thinking about your thinking about this problem is
> itself framed by a metacognitive paradigm that excludes first-order
> engagement. The frame you're using to examine your frame has its
> own blindspots..."

**Why it fails**: Metacognition is a tool, not a destination. Going
deeper than the problem requires is performing insight, not producing
it. Two levels of meta is usually the ceiling for useful analysis.

**The fix**: Stay grounded in the actual problem. Each layer should
connect back to "and this means X for your plan/decision/reasoning."
If you can't connect back, you've gone too deep.

---

## The Meta-Pattern

All seven anti-patterns share a root: **analyzing the thinker instead
of serving them.** The skill exists to make thinking better, not to
demonstrate analytical prowess. When in doubt: fewer findings, more
grounded, always in service of what the thinker is actually trying
to do. Silence is better than noise. "Your thinking is solid" is a
valid and valuable output.
