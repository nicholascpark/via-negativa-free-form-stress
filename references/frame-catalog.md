# Frame Catalog: Common Thinking Frames and Their Blindspots

Every way of thinking illuminates some things and structurally excludes
others. This catalog maps common frames used in plans, decisions, and
reasoning — what they see well and what they can't see at all.

Use this as a reference when performing Layer 3 analysis. The goal is not
to find the "right" frame but to make the thinker aware of what their
current frame renders invisible.

---

## Planning & Execution Frames

### The Sequential Frame
**Sees**: Steps, dependencies, order, milestones.
**Excludes**: Parallel possibilities, emergence, the value of disorder,
feedback loops that change the sequence itself.
**When it fits**: Well-understood tasks with known steps.
**When it doesn't**: Creative work, exploration, anything where the path
is discovered by walking it.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker feels stuck because "I can't do step 3
until step 2 is done" — but step 2 might not actually be a prerequisite;
the sequence is assumed, not required.

### The Optimization Frame
**Sees**: Improvement opportunities, inefficiencies, measurable gains.
**Excludes**: Good enough, diminishing returns, the cost of optimizing,
the possibility that the thing doesn't need to be better.
**When it fits**: Systems with clear metrics and known improvement paths.
**When it doesn't**: Situations where "better" is undefined, or where the
act of optimizing changes what's being optimized.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker can't stop refining. "Just one more pass"
never ends. The work is perpetually almost-done.

### The Completeness Frame
**Sees**: Gaps, missing pieces, what hasn't been addressed.
**Excludes**: The value of incompleteness, strategic omission, the fact
that pursuing completeness often prevents finishing.
**When it fits**: Checklists, compliance, safety-critical systems.
**When it doesn't**: Creative work, MVPs, situations where shipping
something incomplete is better than shipping nothing complete.
**Diagnostic tell**: The plan grows with every review. Nothing is ever
removed. Scope only increases.

## Decision Frames

### The Analytical Frame
**Sees**: Data, comparisons, criteria, evidence, trade-offs.
**Excludes**: Intuition as valid signal, values that can't be quantified,
the possibility that enough data already exists and more won't help.
**When it fits**: Decisions with measurable outcomes and limited variables.
**When it doesn't**: Decisions about identity, relationships, meaning,
or any situation where the most important variables aren't measurable.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker keeps gathering information but the
decision doesn't get clearer. Each new data point is absorbed without
shifting the balance.

### The Binary Frame
**Sees**: Two options, their relative merits, a clean choice.
**Excludes**: The third option, the both option, the neither option, the
"change the question" option, the "decide later" option.
**When it fits**: True either/or choices with genuine mutual exclusivity.
**When it doesn't**: Most decisions, which have more options than the
thinker initially sees.
**Diagnostic tell**: "Should I do A or B?" — when the answer might be C,
or A+B, or "why are you choosing between these two specifically?"

### The Reversibility Frame
**Sees**: Whether decisions can be undone, exit paths, experimental stance.
**Excludes**: The cost of constant reversal, the value of commitment,
the possibility that some decisions need to be treated as irreversible
(even if technically reversible) to get their full benefit.
**When it fits**: Low-stakes experiments, early explorations.
**When it doesn't**: Relationships, values, identity — things that require
commitment to produce their value.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker won't commit to anything because
"I can always change my mind" — but the lack of commitment prevents
them from experiencing what commitment would produce.

## Self-Perception Frames

### The Deficit Frame
**Sees**: What's wrong, broken, missing, inadequate.
**Excludes**: What's working, what's sufficient, strengths being deployed
silently, the possibility that the "problem" is actually fine.
**When it fits**: Genuine diagnosis of real problems.
**When it doesn't**: Self-evaluation, where it becomes self-criticism.
**Diagnostic tell**: Every plan starts with what's wrong. No plan starts
with what's working and how to build on it.

### The Potential Frame
**Sees**: What could be, future possibility, untapped capacity.
**Excludes**: What currently is, present satisfaction, the gap between
potential and actual as itself a source of suffering.
**When it fits**: Growth contexts with genuine runway for change.
**When it doesn't**: Situations where the thinker needs to accept
present reality, not transcend it.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker is perpetually unsatisfied because
they're comparing reality to possibility rather than evaluating
reality on its own terms.

### The Control Frame
**Sees**: What can be managed, directed, influenced, decided.
**Excludes**: What can only be accepted, what emerges without management,
the cost of control itself, what would happen if control were released.
**When it fits**: Situations with genuine agency and clear levers.
**When it doesn't**: Situations involving other people's decisions,
systemic forces, randomness, or outcomes that aren't controllable.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker's plan describes what THEY will do but
not what might happen independent of their actions. The world outside
the thinker's control doesn't appear in the plan.

## Problem Frames

### The Technical Frame
**Sees**: Mechanisms, processes, systems, solutions.
**Excludes**: People, relationships, emotions, politics, culture.
**When it fits**: Problems that are genuinely technical.
**When it doesn't**: Problems that present as technical but are actually
interpersonal, political, or cultural.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker keeps designing solutions that are
technically correct but don't get adopted, because the obstacle is
human, not technical.

### The Narrative Frame
**Sees**: Story, meaning, arc, coherence, causation.
**Excludes**: Randomness, coincidence, meaninglessness, the possibility
that events don't form a story.
**When it fits**: Sense-making, communication, processing experience.
**When it doesn't**: Analysis where imposed narrative creates false
causation. "I failed because X" when X might be unrelated.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker has a compelling story about why things
happened. The story feels true and complete. It might also be wrong.

### The Urgency Frame
**Sees**: What needs to happen now, immediate priorities, crises.
**Excludes**: What's important but not urgent, long-term consequences,
the possibility that the urgency is manufactured or habitual.
**When it fits**: Genuine emergencies with real deadlines.
**When it doesn't**: Chronic states of urgency where everything feels
urgent and nothing important ever gets done.
**Diagnostic tell**: The thinker's plan is entirely reactive. No
proactive or long-term elements appear because the urgent displaces
the important every time.

### The Scarcity Frame
**Sees**: Constraints, limitations, not-enough, competition for resources.
**Excludes**: Abundance, sufficiency, the possibility that constraints
are assumed rather than real, creative solutions that don't require
more resources.
**When it fits**: Genuine resource constraints with hard limits.
**When it doesn't**: Situations where the perceived scarcity is a
framing choice, not an objective condition.
**Diagnostic tell**: Every plan starts from "I don't have enough
[time/money/energy/skill]." No plan starts from "what can I do with
what I have?"

## Using This Catalog

1. Read the thinker's artifact and ask: "Which of these frames is active?"
2. Multiple frames are usually active simultaneously — identify the
   dominant one (the one that most shapes what's visible and invisible).
3. Check: does the frame fit the problem? If there's a mismatch, that's
   the Layer 3 finding.
4. If the frame fits, don't force a finding. Say "the frame is
   appropriate" and move on.
5. When suggesting alternative frames, suggest ONE specific alternative
   that would illuminate something specific. Don't just say "consider
   other perspectives."

### Combining Frames

Sometimes the insight isn't that the frame is wrong but that two frames
are active and contradicting each other:
- Planning frame + reversibility frame = perpetual re-planning
- Optimization frame + completeness frame = infinite refinement
- Deficit frame + potential frame = nothing is ever good enough
- Control frame + urgency frame = exhausting micromanagement

These frame conflicts produce recognizable stuck patterns. The thinker
feels torn not because the decision is hard but because they're applying
two incompatible thinking tools simultaneously.
